The Science

What's really happening when stress makes you hungry

Your body isn't broken — it's doing exactly what it evolved to do. These four systems work together to drive stress-eating, and understanding them is the first step to interrupting the cycle.

🧠

HPA Axis & Cortisol

When you experience stress, the hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal axis floods your body with cortisol. This mobilises blood glucose, promotes fat storage, and powerfully stimulates appetite — especially for calorie-dense comfort foods.

HPA axisCortisol surge
⚖️

Leptin & Ghrelin

Stress suppresses leptin — your "I'm satisfied" signal — while elevating ghrelin, the hunger hormone. Together they create intense food cravings even when you have plenty of energy stored.

Leptin ↓Ghrelin ↑
🎯

Dopamine & Reward

Sweet, fatty, salty foods trigger a dopamine hit in your brain's reward centre. Under stress, this signal is amplified — your brain literally learns to use food as emotional medicine, reinforcing the habit loop.

Dopamine loopReward pathway
📈

Insulin Resistance

Repeated cortisol-driven glucose spikes reduce your cells' sensitivity to insulin over time. Stress-eating high-glycaemic foods compounds this risk, increasing the likelihood of metabolic syndrome.

Glucose dysregulationMetabolic risk
The Cycle

How a moment of stress becomes a habit

Four steps — and each one makes the next harder to interrupt. Until you see them clearly.

1
Stressor perceived — your brain flags a threat. The HPA axis activates within seconds, and cortisol starts rising.
↓
2
Hormones shift — cortisol climbs, leptin falls, ghrelin rises. Your appetite surges — even if you ate an hour ago.
↓
3
You reach for food — a dopamine hit from something sweet or salty provides brief relief. The stress response eases a little.
↓
4
The pattern hardens — your brain files this away: "stress → food → better." Each repetition deepens the groove. This is where StressReset steps in.
